# 01 — Writings and Publications

> **Ray Dalio** (born August 8, 1949) — Founder of Bridgewater Associates, the world's largest hedge fund. Author of five books including three #1 New York Times bestsellers. His writings span investment philosophy, macroeconomics, corporate management, and the cyclical nature of world history.

This document catalogs Dalio's published works in chronological order. Each entry summarizes the core thesis, key principles, and links to the source. All content is from original English sources.

---

## 1. How the Economic Machine Works (2007 / 2012)

**Formats:** Animated YouTube video (2012, 30 min) + Free PDF (2007) + Transcript

**Links:**
- [YouTube Video](https://youtu.be/PHe0bXAIuk0) — The original animated explanation (175M+ views)
- [Economic Principles Website](https://www.economicprinciples.org/) — Free PDF download
- [Transcript](https://www.economicprinciples.org/how-the-economic-machine-works) — Full text version

**Core Thesis:**
The economy works like a simple machine. By understanding three fundamental forces — (1) productivity growth, (2) the short-term debt cycle, and (3) the long-term debt cycle — you can understand where the economy is headed. Most economic suffering comes from not understanding how the machine works.

**Key Principles:**
- The economy is the sum of all transactions made in society
- One person's spending is another person's income — therefore spending drives the economy
- Credit (debt) creates spending cycles that drive short-term and long-term economic fluctuations
- When debt rises faster than income, and income rises faster than productivity, things go wrong
- Productivity growth is the foundation of long-term wealth; debt cycles cause volatility around the productivity trendline

**Three Forces Explained:**
1. **Productivity growth** — The long-term upward trend (~2% per year). This is what makes us wealthier over time. Knowledge and technology compound.
2. **Short-term debt cycle** — 5-8 years. The traditional business cycle driven by central bank interest rate policy. When the economy slows, central banks cut rates to stimulate borrowing. This creates a boom-bust pattern.
3. **Long-term debt cycle** — 50-75 years. Each successive short-term cycle leaves debt levels higher than before. Over decades, debt accumulates until it becomes unsustainable. Then a "deleveraging" occurs.

**Historical Significance:**
This was Dalio's first published work and became the foundation for all his subsequent books. The 2007 essay accurately predicted the 2008 financial crisis. The 2012 animated video is one of the most-watched economics explainers of all time.

**Notes:** Originally published as a free PDF titled "How the Economic Machine Works: A Template for Understanding What is Happening Now" (2007). Later expanded into *Economic Principles* (2015, ~300 pages). The concepts in this 30-minute video laid the groundwork for *Big Debt Crises* and *The Changing World Order*.

## 2. Principles: Life & Work (2017)

**Publisher:** Simon & Schuster

**Links:**
- [Amazon](https://amzn.to/3TkXXoL)
- [Principles.com](https://www.principles.com/)
- [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/34536488-principles)

**ISBN:** 978-1501124020

**Core Thesis:**
A comprehensive framework of life and work principles developed over 40+ years at Bridgewater. The book argues that success comes from having clear, codified principles — decision-making rules derived from understanding cause-and-effect relationships — and that radical truth and radical transparency are the most powerful tools for personal and organizational growth.

**Background:**
Originally self-published as a 123-page PDF in 2011 solely for Bridgewater employees and clients. It was downloaded millions of times. Bill Gates wrote the foreword for the commercial edition.

**Structure (Three Parts):**

**Part 1: Where I'm Coming From** — Dalio's personal story
- Growing up in middle-class Long Island
- Investing at age 12 (bought Northeast Airlines)
- Founding Bridgewater and the 1982 near-bankruptcy
- Building the world's largest hedge fund

**Part 2: Life Principles**

| Principle | Explanation | Key Quote |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Embrace Reality and Deal With It | Denial is the enemy of progress | "Dreams + Reality + Determination = A Successful Life" |
| Pain + Reflection = Progress | Discomfort signals a learning opportunity | "My painful mistakes shifted me from having a perspective of 'I know I'm right' to 'How do I know I'm right?'" |
| Be Radically Open-Minded | Your own mind is your biggest obstacle | "The two biggest barriers to good decision making are your ego and your blind spots" |
| Understand That People Are Wired Very Differently | Different brains produce different thinking | Learn your personality type — understand others' wiring |
| The 5-Step Process | Universal framework for achievement | See below |
| Look Down From Above | Get the higher perspective | Don't let details obscure patterns |
| Weigh Decision Making with Believability | Not all opinions are equal | Trust track record over credentials |

**The 5-Step Process:**
1. **Set clear goals** — Know what you want. Prioritize. Don't confuse goals with desires.
2. **Identify problems** — Be honest about what stands between you and your goals. Don't tolerate problems.
3. **Diagnose root causes** — Go deep. "What is the root cause?" (not "what is the problem")
4. **Design a plan** — Design the machine that will produce the outcome you want.
5. **Execute** — Push through. Self-discipline and good habits.

**Part 3: Work Principles**
- Build an **idea meritocracy** — the best ideas win
- Implement **radical truth** and **radical transparency**
- **Believability-weight** decision-making
- Recognize that people are the most important part of any machine
- **Systematize** everything

**Reception:**
- #1 New York Times Bestseller
- Amazon's #1 Business Book of 2017
- CNBC's 13 Best Business Books of 2017
- Time Magazine feature

**What Critics Say:**
- Praised for its systematic approach to management
- Some critics say the principles are too rigid for creative work
- The "radical transparency" culture has been criticized as creating a high-pressure environment

## 3. Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises (2018)

**Publisher:** Self-published (free PDF originally) / Simon & Schuster (commercial)

**Links:**
- [Free PDF](https://www.principles.com/Principles-For-Navigating-Big-Debt-Crises-By-Ray-Dalio.pdf)
- [Amazon](https://amzn.to/3fRstsW)
- [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/41436447-principles-for-navigating-big-debt-crises)

**ISBN:** 978-1501124044

**Core Thesis:**
A complete macroeconomic education in three parts. Debt crises are inevitable due to human nature — the tendency to borrow too much during good times. Understanding the mechanics of the "big debt cycle" is essential for navigating economic turmoil. The book provides a template for how debt crises unfold and how policymakers can manage deleveraging effectively.

**Structure:**

**Part 1: The Archetypal Big Debt Cycle (50 pages)**
- The seven phases of a debt crisis
- The "beautiful deleveraging" — using the right mix of debt reduction tools
- Key metrics to track: debt-to-income ratios, output gaps, credit spreads

**Part 2: Three Detailed Case Studies**
1. **Germany (1918-1924)** — The classic hyperinflation case. How the Weimar Republic printed money to pay war reparations, leading to the complete destruction of the German mark.
2. **United States (1929-1937)** — The Great Depression. How the Fed failed to provide enough liquidity, deepening the crisis. Eventually resolved through massive fiscal stimulus (WWII).
3. **United States (2007-2011)** — The 2008 Financial Crisis. Dalio's personal experience. How aggressive central bank intervention and "QE" created a beautiful deleveraging.

**Part 3: 48 Additional Historical Debt Crises**
Compressed case studies of debt crises from around the world and across history, organized by type: inflation crises, deflation crises, currency crises, and sovereign defaults.

**Key Principles:**
- **Debt crises are inevitable** — Human nature drives borrowing beyond sustainable levels
- **"Buying something you can't afford means spending more than you make. You're not just borrowing from your lender; you are borrowing from your future self."**
- **Beautiful deleveraging** — If you mix the four deleveraging tools (debt restructuring, austerity, wealth transfers, and money printing) in the right proportions, you can work through the crisis without causing depression
- **Debt is not inherently bad** — It depends on whether the borrowed money generates sufficient returns to repay
- **Central banks can't fix everything** — Once interest rates hit zero, monetary policy loses its primary tool

**The Four Deleveraging Tools:**
1. **Debt restructuring** — Creditors accept losses
2. **Spending cuts** — Austerity
3. **Wealth transfers** — Tax increases on rich
4. **Money printing** — Central bank creates money, buys debt ("QE")

**Notes:** 450+ pages. Contains dozens of charts and data exhibits. Dalio originally gave the book away for free to anyone who signed up for his email list.

## 5. Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order: Why Nations Succeed and Fail (2021)

**Publisher:** Simon & Schuster

**Links:**
- [Amazon](https://amzn.to/3Une9Y9)
- [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Changing_World_Order)
- [YouTube Animation](https://youtu.be/xguam0TKMw8) — 43-minute companion video

**ISBN:** 978-1982164799

**Core Thesis:**
A sweeping historical analysis of how empires rise and fall. The same "Big Cycle" has repeated across 500 years of history, driven by a consistent set of determinants. The US is now in decline (late-stage); China is on the rise. Understanding this cycle is essential for investors, policymakers, and anyone who wants to navigate the coming decades.

**Background:**
Dalio spent years studying the rise and fall of the Dutch, British, and American empires. He examined currencies, debt levels, education, innovation, military strength, and dozens of other factors across 500+ years.

**Structure (Three Parts):**

**Part 1: How the World Works**
- The Big Cycle framework
- 17 determinants of power
- How nations become successful through money, credit, debt, and economic activity
- The role of "internal order" (domestic peace) and "external order" (international peace)

**Part 2: Three Case Studies**
1. **The Dutch Empire (1600-1800)** — How a small trading nation became the world's dominant power through financial innovation, trade, and naval supremacy, then declined.
2. **The British Empire (1700-1950)** — How industrial revolution, naval power, and financial markets made Britain dominant, and how two world wars and colonial decline ended the empire.
3. **The United States Empire (1850-present)** — From civil war to world power to Stage 5.

**Part 3: The Future**
- China's emergence as a rival superpower
- US-China relations through the Big Cycle lens
- Scenarios for how the current cycle might resolve

**The Six Stages of the Big Cycle:**

| Stage | What Happens | Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | New order begins — a clear winner establishes rules | Short |
| 2 | Resource allocation system is built | Long |
| 3 | Peace and prosperity — the golden era | Long (one generation+) |
| 4 | Excess and bubble — overconfidence, debt accumulation, wealth gap widening | Variable |
| 5 | **Distribution conflicts** — internal disorder, populism rising | Short |
| 6 | Civil war or revolution — the breakdown | Variable, then back to Stage 1 |

**The 17 Determinants of Power:**
1. Education — quality and accessibility
2. Innovation/Technology — rate of invention and adoption
3. Competitiveness — cost and quality of production
4. Military strength — spending and capability
5. Trade — share of global trade
6. Economic output — share of global GDP
7. Infrastructure — quality of roads, ports, internet
8. Investment — rate of capital formation
9. Cost competitiveness — labor costs, productivity
10. Reserves — currency reserve status
11. Capital markets — depth and liquidity
12. Culture — work ethic, trust, rule of law
13. Governance — quality of leadership and institutions
14. Rule of law — consistent enforcement
15. Luck — geographic advantages, resource endowment
16. Geography — natural defenses, access to trade routes
17. Character of the people — resilience, adaptability

**Current Assessment:**
The US scores highest overall but is declining on several key metrics (debt, infrastructure, education, political division). China is rising rapidly and is competitive or superior on many measures.

**Key Quotes:**
- "No system of government, no economic system, no currency, and no empire lasts forever, yet almost everyone is surprised and ruined when they fail."
- "Global superpowers start with a focus on improving education which, following a long series of steps, will lead to that nation's currency increasing in value."

**Reception:**
- New York Times Bestseller
- Called "prescient" by many reviewers (especially regarding US-China tensions)
- Critics note the framework is broad and sometimes oversimplifies complex histories

## 7. How Countries Go Broke: The Big Cycle (June 2025) 🆕

**Publisher:** Avid Reader Press (Simon & Schuster)

**Links:**
- [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/How-Countries-Broke-Principles-Navigating/dp/1668149486)
- [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_Countries_Go_Broke)
- [Simon & Schuster](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/How-Countries-Go-Broke/Ray-Dalio/Principles/9781501124068)

**ISBN:** 978-1501124068

**Publication Date:** June 3, 2025

**Core Thesis:**
An urgent warning about sovereign debt crises. Most people don't understand the mechanics of how countries go broke. The book provides a detailed template for the "big debt cycle" — how the accumulation of short-term debt cycles results in a larger debt crisis that most investors overlook. It also provides a practical playbook for policymakers to manage the crisis.

**Background:**
Dalio says this is his most urgent book. He wrote it because he believes we are approaching a critical point in the debt cycle and most people — including policymakers — don't understand the mechanics. Advance copies became "a hot read in Washington" according to the New York Times.

**Key Concepts:**

**The 9 Stages of a Debt Crisis:**
1. **The beginning** — Strong economy, manageable debt levels
2. **The bubble** — Debt begins growing faster than income; asset prices rise
3. **The top** — Debt peaks relative to GDP; credit spreads widen
4. **The tightening** — Central bank raises rates to fight inflation; cracks appear
5. **The reversal** — Asset prices fall; the self-reinforcing downturn begins
6. **The deleveraging** — Debt restructuring, defaults, bailouts
7. **The depression** — Economic contraction, high unemployment
8. **The reflation** — Central bank prints money, buys assets (QE)
9. **The new beginning** — The cycle resets; a new monetary order emerges

**Warning Signs:**
- Government printing more money
- Selling more debt than can be bought back
- Rising debt-service-to-income ratios
- Foreign investors reducing holdings of government debt
- Central banks becoming the buyer of last resort (monetizing debt)
- Political inability to raise taxes or cut spending

**The "Internal Order" Framework:**
Dalio introduces the concept of "internal order" — the social and political stability of a nation depends on:
1. The wealth and income of its citizens
2. The gap between the rich and poor
3. Shared values and culture
4. Trust in institutions
5. The rule of law

When these deteriorate, internal disorder follows, making it harder to manage debt crises.

**Core Argument:**
The United States and other developed nations are now showing multiple signs of being in the late stages of the big debt cycle. The only question is how the deleveraging will play out — whether it will be "beautiful" (managed well) or "ugly" (default, hyperinflation, civil conflict).

**Reception:**
- **#1 New York Times Bestseller**
- **Henry Paulson Jr. (former Treasury Secretary):** "This book is a gift to humanity…Ray provides a solution to what is the biggest and most certain threat to our prosperity."
- **New York Times:** "Advance copies of Ray Dalio's new book about how countries go broke have become a hot read in Washington."
- **Kenneth Rogoff (Harvard):** Praised Dalio's critique of US/Japan fiscal policies but noted the book fails to engage with prior academic works like *This Time Is Different* (2009)
- **Richard Morrison (The Daily Economy):** Applauded historical analysis but found the book "overly long and contains internal contradictions"
- **Keith Wade (Society of Professional Economists):** "Timely contribution amid rising global debt levels" with great data, but criticized lack of citations

## Core Principles (Recurring Themes Across All Writings)

Dalio's writings are remarkably consistent across two decades. The following 12 principles or themes appear across virtually all his published works:

1. **The Machine Metaphor** — Everything (economy, markets, organizations) works like a machine that can be understood and optimized
2. **Cycle Over Line** — History doesn't move in a straight line; it cycles through predictable patterns (debt cycles, political cycles, empire cycles)
3. **Radical Truth & Transparency** — The most effective way to operate is to face reality directly and operate without hidden agendas
4. **Pain + Reflection = Progress** — Discomfort is the entry point for learning and growth
5. **Believability-Weighting** — Not all opinions are equal; weight them by the track record of the person holding them
6. **Systematize Decision-Making** — Convert every decision into a principle that can be recorded, tested, and improved
7. **Understand Before Acting** — Deep understanding of cause-effect relationships must precede action
8. **Diversification Against the Unknown** — Since no one can predict the future perfectly, diversify risk across uncorrelated assets
9. **The 5-Step Process** — Goals → Problems → Diagnosis → Design → Execution
10. **The Two Yous** — Your rational brain must manage your emotional brain
11. **The Big Cycle** — All nations go through a predictable 6-stage cycle
12. **The Big Debt Cycle** — All economies go through a predictable debt accumulation and deleveraging cycle
