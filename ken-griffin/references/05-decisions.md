## SUCCESS CASES

---

## Case 1: Starting Citadel at Age 22 (1990)

**The Decision:** After one year trading at Glenwood Capital (70% return), Griffin founded Citadel with $4.6 million.

**The Risk:** No safety net. Zero institutional backing. A 22-year-old competing against 100-year-old banks.

**The Advice:** Frank Meyer told him to build a multi-strategy platform, not a single-strategy fund.

**Result:** Citadel now manages $68B+. Griffin net worth: $51.2B.

---

## Case 2: The 1998 Capital Lockup Decision

**The Decision:** Restructured redemption terms to require a two-year lockup or penalties for early withdrawal.

**The Timing:** Weeks later, LTCM collapsed. Other funds were forced sellers. Citadel was a buyer.

**Result:** Finished 1998 up 30%. AUM grew from $2B to $6B by 2001.

**Lesson:** "Risk management starts before the storm hits."

---

## Case 3: Hiring the Russian Rocket Scientist (Early 1990s)

**The Decision:** Hired a literal rocket scientist from the Russian space program.

**The Context:** Wall Street mocked the hire. A Bear Stearns contact called to ask what Griffin was thinking.

**The Insight:** The skills to build rockets under government budget constraints overlap with building robust quantitative models.

**Result:** The firm that mocked the hire is now out of business. Citadel's quant edge began here.

---

## Case 4: Building Citadel Securities (2002)

**The Decision:** Founded a market-making business alongside the hedge fund.

**The Risk:** Potential conflicts of interest. Regulatory scrutiny. Operational complexity.

**The Result:** Citadel Securities now executes a significant share of U.S. retail trading volume. A key profit center.

**Controversy:** The connection between the two entities came under scrutiny during the 2021 GameStop episode.

## Case 6: Surviving 2008 (The Near-Death Experience)

**The Situation:** Citadel was leveraged 7:1. Losses hit 55%. The firm was losing "hundreds of millions of dollars each week."

**The Decision:**
- Suspended redemptions to prevent forced liquidation
- Griffin and partners injected $500M of personal capital
- Negotiated favorable financing terms (learned from LTCM study)

**The Result:** Recovered with 62% return in 2009. Crossed high-water mark by January 2012.

**The Lesson:** "We were overly confident that we could weather any financial storm."

---

## Case 7: The Amaranth Acquisition (2006)

**The Decision:** Acquired Amaranth's entire energy portfolio at a steep discount after its $6B loss on natural gas.

**The Execution:** Quick, decisive. Citadel absorbed both the assets and the talent.

**Result:** Strengthened Citadel's commodities operation significantly.

---

## Case 8: The E-Trade Investment (2007)

**The Decision:** Invested $2.5 billion in E-Trade during the credit crisis.

**What They Got:** E-Trade's securitized subprime mortgage portfolio, CDOs, second lien loans, plus a 19.99% equity stake and board seat.

**The Exit:** Position sold in 2013 at a significant profit.

---

## Case 9: The Multi-Manager Pod Model (Post-2008)

**The Decision:** Reorganized into discrete semi-autonomous pods after 2008.

**The Structure:**
- Each pod: 4-8 professionals, sector specialist, market-neutral
- Central Risk: monitors all positions in real-time
- Capital allocation: more capital to producing pods, less to underperforming ones
- Two consecutive years of underperformance → team dissolved

**Result:** Hit #1 in cumulative gains by 2022.

**Citadel's three equity units:**
1. Citadel Global Equities (original)
2. Surveyor Capital (2008)
3. Ashler Capital (2018)

---

## Case 10: Moving to Florida (2022)

**The Decision:** Moved Citadel's HQ from Chicago to Miami.

**Why:** "Chicago is a great city with a broken government."

**The Scale:** Massively expanded office footprint. In January 2026, committed $180M for Wynwood office building.

**The Controversy:** Backlash from Chicago politicians and residents.

---

## Case 11: Stopping Capital Raising (2019)

**The Decision:** Citadel stopped actively raising new capital despite massive investor demand.

**The Reason:** "We couldn't find enough opportunities to put additional money to work."

**The Strategy:** Return excess profits to clients annually. Prioritize returns per dollar over AUM.

**Result:** More than $25B returned to investors in distributions.

## FAILED CASES / NEAR-MISSES

### 2008 Near-Collapse

The biggest failure was the 2008 crisis itself. Griffin acknowledged the firm was "overly confident."

**Lesson:** Multiple strategies don't protect when correlations go to 1.

### Aragon Global Management Investment

Griffin was the lead investor in his then-wife Anne Dias's hedge fund, Aragon Global Management. He lost 20% of his investment.

**Lesson:** Mixing personal relationships and investment capital carries risk.

### The iStar / Investment Banking Attempt

Citadel attempted to create an investment banking unit and a reinsurance business, but neither achieved the success of the core hedge fund and market-making operations.
