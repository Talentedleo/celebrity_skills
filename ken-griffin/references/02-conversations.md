## 8. The 2010 Year-End Letter — Key Excerpts

**Source:** [NYT DealBook](https://archive.nytimes.com/dealbook.nytimes.com/2010/12/27/citadel-founder-offers-thanks-apology-on-anniversary/)

On the four lessons from 20 years of building Citadel:
> 1. Hire the best people and create a culture of opportunity.
> 2. Take calculated risks.
> 3. Embrace opportunities.
> 4. Never lose sight of what is important.

On the decision to suspend redemptions in 2008:
> "One of the most difficult decisions that I ever made was suspending redemptions in 2008. I knew it had to be done to protect our balance sheet, preserve our investors' capital and to maximize short-term performance."

On the lessons of 2008:
> "While our boldness has served us well, we are not blind to the lessons of 2008. We were overly confident that we could weather any financial storm."

## 9. Anecdote: The Harvard Satellite Dish

**Source:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_C._Griffin)

Despite the ban on running businesses from campus, Griffin convinced Harvard administrators to let him install a satellite dish on the roof of Cabot House dormitory to receive real-time stock quotes.

His first fund launched in 1987, days after his 19th birthday, with $265,000. The fund opened into Black Monday — he had built short positions and profited from the crash.

## 10. Anecdote: The Russian Rocket Scientist

**Source:** [Young & Calculated](https://youngandcalculated.substack.com/p/citadel-the-full-story-behind-the)

In the early 1990s, Griffin hired a Russian rocket scientist — someone who had literally built rockets for the Russian government. A contact at Bear Stearns called to ask what Griffin was thinking.

> "I knew nothing about running a business. But I knew I could trade."

The rocket scientist helped build pricing models that gave Citadel its edge in quantitative strategies. The bank that mocked the hire is now out of business; Citadel is one of the world's most important financial firms.

## 12. Anecdote: The 1998 LTCM Crisis Decision

**Source:** [Quartr](https://quartr.com/insights/company-research/citadel-relentless-optimization-at-global-scale)

In 1998, Griffin restructured Citadel's redemption terms, requiring a minimum two-year lockup or quarterly liquidity with penalties. Weeks later, LTCM collapsed. Citadel was a buyer while competitors were forced sellers.

The fund finished 1998 up 30%.

Griffin on risk management:
> "Risk management starts before the storm hits."

---

## 13. Anecdote: The Grandmother Investor

**Source:** [NYT DealBook](https://archive.nytimes.com/dealbook.nytimes.com/2010/12/27/citadel-founder-offers-thanks-apology-on-anniversary/)

In his 2010 year-end letter, Griffin told the story of his grandmother, Genevieve Gratz, who was one of Citadel's first investors. After his grandfather died, she was approached by businessmen who offered a modest amount for his fuel oil distribution business.

> "Surely, they assumed, a widow with two daughters to raise and a farm to run would be eager to sell. They were wrong."

She sold the business years later for many times more.

> "On the 20th anniversary of Citadel's founding, and six months after her death at age 98, I am confident our performance has justified her belief in Citadel. It also demonstrates the timelessness of the values and principles that she passed on to me."

---

## 14. Anecdote: The 3 AM Trade (Sowood Capital, 2007)

**Source:** [Young & Calculated](https://youngandcalculated.substack.com/p/citadel-the-full-story-behind-the)

When Sowood Capital was collapsing during the credit dislocation of 2007, Citadel assembled a team that made an offer to purchase the distressed assets at 3:30 AM — while other firms had stopped working. The trade closed.

This became emblematic of Citadel's approach: work when others aren't, and move with speed when others hesitate.

---

## 15. Anecdote: Recruiting from Collapsed Firms

**Source:** [Quartr](https://quartr.com/insights/company-research/citadel-relentless-optimization-at-global-scale)

Griffin has consistently recruited talent from collapsing competitors:

- **2001 (Enron):** Recruited energy traders and quantitative researchers the day after the bankruptcy filing
- **2006 (Amaranth):** Acquired the energy portfolio and talent at a steep discount
- **2007 (Sowood):** Purchased distressed assets and hired the team
- **2008 (Lehman/Merrill):** Hired top talent as they were laid off

## 16. Anecdote: The 500 Stress Tests

**Source:** [Quartr](https://quartr.com/insights/company-research/citadel-relentless-optimization-at-global-scale)

In the aftermath of 2008, Citadel's risk management infrastructure ran **500 stress tests daily**. This infrastructure, combined with palatable financing terms negotiated with prime brokers after studying LTCM's collapse, is what saved the firm in 2008.

## 21. Harvard 1986 Sun-Sentinel Interview (High School)

**Source:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_C._Griffin)

At 18, before starting Harvard, Griffin predicted in a local newspaper interview that computer programming jobs would decline significantly — while he was himself in a computer programming competition.

This early contrarian thinking — predicting the opposite of the prevailing trend while directly participating in it — foreshadowed his career.
