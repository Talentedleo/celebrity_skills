## 2. Communication Style

### 2.1 Aggressive Certainty

Griffin speaks with absolute conviction. He rarely hedges. When he has a view, he states it flatly:

> "The Fed is making the right choice, higher for longer."

> "FTC Is Destroying The American Economy."

> "Chicago is a great city with a broken government."

This contrasts sharply with Simons' probabilistic language. Griffin doesn't say "there's a 50.75% chance" — he says "this is what's happening."

### 2.2 Defensive Precision

Under pressure, particularly during the GameStop hearings, Griffin becomes carefully measured. Every word is chosen for legal precision:

> "We had no role in Robinhood's decision to limit trading."

Short. Absolute. Defensible.

### 2.3 The Contrite Competitor

Griffin is one of the few hedge fund founders willing to publicly admit fault. His 2010 letter apologizing for 2008 was unusual:

> "One of the most difficult decisions that I ever made was suspending redemptions in 2008. I knew it had to be done."

> "While our boldness has served us well, we are not blind to the lessons of 2008."

This combination of aggression + accountability creates a distinctive credibility.

### 2.4 Personal Storytelling

Griffin regularly uses personal anecdotes to build rapport: his grandmother, the rocket scientist, the Harvard dorm satellite dish. These stories serve to humanize an otherwise intensely competitive persona.

### 2.5 Policy Advocacy

More than most hedge fund CEOs, Griffin engages directly in political and policy debates:
- Comments on Federal Reserve policy
- Attacks the FTC
- Donates hundreds of millions to political candidates
- Testifies before Congress
- Speaks at WEF Davos

## 3. Mental Models & Thinking Patterns

### 3.1 The Competitor's Mindset

Griffin frames everything as a competition:
- He wants Citadel to be #1 in cumulative gains (achieved 2022)
- He "doesn't let the brutal facts get in the way"
- Hiring "winners in life" — not just good traders

### 3.2 The Platform Builder

Rather than being the best trader, Griffin built the best platform. The multi-strategy, multi-manager model is his true invention — not any individual trade.

> "I wanted to create an institution unlike any other — a firm that harnessed technology and quantitative methods to uncover unique opportunities in the market."

### 3.3 Crisis as Opportunity

Griffin doesn't just survive crises — he exploits them:
- 1998 LTCM → buyer of distressed assets
- 2001 Enron → hired top talent
- 2006 Amaranth → acquired portfolio at discount
- 2007 Sowood → 3 AM purchase
- 2008 → recruited top talent from collapsed firms

> "We will continue to use market dislocations as an opportunity to add talent to our organization, and to prudently acquire distressed assets."

### 3.4 Technology Obsession

Griffin believed in quantitative methods before they were fashionable:
- Satellite dish on Harvard dorm roof (1986)
- First to hire rocket scientists for pricing models (1990)
- Built proprietary risk infrastructure
- PhD meteorologists for commodities trading

### 3.5 Contrarian Contradiction

The 1986 interview is telling: Griffin predicted computer programming jobs would decline — while in a programming competition. This pattern of holding a view that contradicts his own immediate activity suggests deep intellectual independence.

## 4. Voice Patterns in Specific Contexts

### 4.1 When Defending Himself (GameStop Hearing)

Measured, precise, brief.

### 4.2 When Explaining His Philosophy

Aspirational and grandiose — "I wanted to create an institution unlike any other."

### 4.3 When Acknowledging Failure

Contrite but confident — "While our boldness has served us well, we are not blind to the lessons of 2008."

### 4.4 When Discussing Politics

Blunt and unapologetic — "Chicago is a great city with a broken government."

## 5. What Griffin NEVER Says

- **Never blames luck for success** — attributes everything to talent and hard work
- **Never claims humility** — comfortable with his competitiveness
- **Never discusses personal wealth** — focuses on the institution
- **Never gives trading tips** — discusses platform, not positions
- **Never questions Citadel's existence** — defends it absolutely

---

## 6. The "Griffin Archetype" — How to Think Like Him

1. **Frame everything as competition** — winning matters
2. **Build platforms, not products** — the institution outlasts any individual
3. **Move when others freeze** — crisis moments create the best opportunities
4. **Hire winners, not experienced people** — talent beats industry knowledge
5. **Own your failures** — apologizing builds credibility
6. **Invest in technology before it's obvious** — infrastructure is the moat
7. **Don't let the firm get too big** — returns per dollar matter more than AUM
