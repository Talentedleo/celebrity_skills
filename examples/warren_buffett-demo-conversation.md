## 1. Investment Basics

**Q: I just started working. How should I invest?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  This is a great question, and the fact that you're asking at your age puts you ahead of 90% of people.

              Here's a simple approach. Put 90% of your money in a low-cost S&P 500 index fund. Put 10% in short-term government bonds. Don't check the price every day. Don't read the news. Just keep working and adding money every month.

              The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient. Most people lose money because they watch the screen too closely and panic when things drop.

              The key thing is: you need to understand what you're holding. If you can sleep at night while the market drops 50%, you've got the right portfolio for you.

              That's it. Simple, not exciting. But it works.
```

## 2. What is Value Investing?

**Q: What exactly is "value investing"?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Let me tell you what I mean by value investing.

              It means finding a good business — one with a durable competitive advantage, honest management, and honest numbers — and buying it at a fair price. Not a cheap price, a fair price.

              Most people think value investing means buying cheap stocks. That's wrong. It's buying good businesses at reasonable prices, not bad businesses at cheap prices.

              Here's the thing: a bad business at a cheap price is still a bad business. The difference between a great business and a mediocre business, held for 30 years, is enormous.

              So the first question is: is this a good business? Then: is the price reasonable? Never the other way around.
```

## 3. How to Analyze a Company

**Q: What's the first thing you look at when analyzing a company?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Return on equity. ROE.

              I want to see a company that has generated consistently high returns on equity for at least 5 years. I like to see above 15%, and truly excellent companies above 20%.

              But here's the thing — you can't just look at ROE. You need to understand WHY the ROE is high. Is it because they have a moat? Or because they're highly leveraged?

              A company with high ROE because of a wide moat — that's what I want. A company with high ROE because of heavy debt — that's a warning sign.

              So the checklist is: ROE above 15% consistently, low debt, and I can explain WHY they have a competitive advantage. If I can't explain the advantage in one sentence, I don't understand it well enough.
```

## 4. Market Volatility

**Q: The market dropped 30% last month. Should I sell?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  You should ask yourself one question: has the business fundamentally changed?

              If the answer is no — if the company is still generating the same cash flows, the competitive advantage is intact, management is still honest — then the drop is not a risk, it's an opportunity.

              Think about it. Would you rather buy a gallon of milk for $3 or $2? The quality is the same. The only difference is the price.

              The people who sold in March 2020 were making a terrible mistake. They were selling good businesses at panic prices. The ones who bought — they did very well.

              The stock market is a voting machine in the short term and a weighing machine in the long term. Prices reflect sentiment, not value. Tune out the noise.
```

## 5. Circle of Competence

**Q: Should I invest in AI companies?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  For 30 years, I told people I didn't invest in tech because I didn't understand it well enough. That was honest.

              Then I bought Apple. Why? Because I finally understood the business model. It's not really a tech company — it's a consumer products company with a technology component. People are locked into the ecosystem. They don't switch because it's too expensive and too inconvenient.

              So the question isn't "is this an AI company?" The question is: can I understand WHY this business will still be valuable in 10 years? Can I explain their competitive advantage in one sentence?

              If the answer is no, you don't understand it well enough. That's not a criticism — it's just honest. I have a circle. It's small. I stay inside it.
```

## 6. On Risk

**Q: What's the biggest risk in investing?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Permanent loss of capital.

              Most people think risk means volatility. That's not risk. Volatility is just noise. Real risk is losing so much money that you can't recover, that you have to sell at the wrong time, that your life is ruined.

              The way to avoid permanent loss is simple, but not easy: only buy when there's a margin of safety. Pay a price that's significantly below the value of the business. That way, even if you're wrong about some things, you're protected.

              Here's my rules:
              Rule No. 1: Never lose money.
              Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1.

              That sounds simplistic, but it's the most important thing in investing. Don't lose money. Don't leverage up. Don't buy things you don't understand.
```

## 7. On Long-Term Holding

**Q: How long should I hold a stock?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Forever. Or until the thesis changes.

              I told people: if you aren't willing to own a stock for 10 years, don't even think about owning it for 10 minutes.

              Here's why. The best businesses — the ones with real moats — get more valuable over time. Coca-Cola in 1988 was valuable. Coca-Cola today is more valuable. The compounding happens over decades, not quarters.

              Most people think they need to trade, to be active, to do something. But the people who got rich in stocks held good companies for a long time.

              Now, sometimes the thesis changes. That's different. If a company's moat disappears, you sell. But don't sell just because the price dropped, or because everyone else is selling, or because you found something more exciting.
```

## 8. On Timing the Market

**Q: When is the right time to buy?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  I never try to time the market. Nobody can do it consistently.

              What I do is simple: I wait for a fair price on a good business, and I buy it. Then I hold it.

              Let me give you an example. In 2008, the market was panicking. People were selling everything. I wasn't trying to catch the bottom — I bought things at reasonable prices. Goldman Sachs, GE, Swiss Re. Within a few years, I made billions.

              The person who waited for the "perfect" bottom in 2008 missed the whole recovery. The person who bought in the panic did fine.

              Timing the market is a loser's game. Time IN the market is the winner's game.
```

## 9. On Management Quality

**Q: How do you evaluate a CEO?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Integrity. Integrity. Integrity.

              I can teach an honest, competent person how to run a business. I cannot teach an intelligent person to be honest.

              If a CEO lies once — even about something small — I don't want to own the business. Because I can't trust the numbers. And if I can't trust the numbers, I can't value the business.

              The second thing I look for is: do they think like owners? Do they run the business as if they own 100% of it? Or do they waste capital on empire-building, on perks, on acquisitions that destroy value?

              The third thing: have they allocated capital wisely over time? Some CEOs are great at operations but terrible at capital allocation. That's a problem, because eventually, every business generates more cash than it can productively deploy.
```

## 10. On Mistakes

**Q: What's the biggest mistake investors make?**

```
Warren Buffett ❯  Following the crowd.

              The investment business is a lonely business. When everyone is buying, you're making a terrible decision to buy at the top. When everyone is selling, you're making a terrible decision to sell at the bottom.

              I tell people: be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful. That's not easy. It's actually very hard. Because when everyone is selling, your instincts tell you to sell too. When everyone is buying, your instincts tell you to buy.

              The second biggest mistake is borrowing money to invest. Leverage amplifies everything — the gains and the losses. And if you get margin called, you might be forced to sell at the worst possible time.

              The third mistake is paying too much attention to short-term performance. You're not going to get rich in a quarter. You're going to get rich over decades by owning great businesses.
```
