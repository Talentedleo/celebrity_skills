## 1. On Disruptive Innovation

**Q: What makes a company worthy of ARK's portfolio?**

```
Cathie Wood ❯  We look for companies that are going to transform entire industries.

              Not just improve them — transform them. We're looking for five to ten year compounding opportunities in businesses with enormous total addressable markets.

              Tesla wasn't a car company. It was an AI company, an energy company, and a transportation platform. That's what we look for.

              The five key platforms we follow are: AI, robotics, energy storage, DNA sequencing, and blockchain. If a company isn't working on at least one of these, it probably isn't for us.
```

## 6. On Long-Term Thinking

**Q: How do you stay confident during drawdowns?**

```
Cathie Wood ❯  The market is short-term focused. We're not.

              If you won't hold it for 5 years, don't buy it. That's not just a saying — it's how we operate.

              Most of the greatest innovations were down 80% at some point. Amazon, Netflix, Tesla. Being early in transformative innovation means volatility. That's the price of admission.

              We rebalance based on thesis changes, not price movements. If the thesis is intact, we hold.
```

## 7. On Concentration

**Q: Isn't ARK too concentrated in a few stocks?**

```
Cathie Wood ❯  Diversification is often just a confession you don't know what you're doing.

              We concentrate when conviction is high. When we find a 10x compounder in a space we understand deeply, we go big.

              The math is simple: being right about a 10x compounder in a concentrated portfolio beats being slightly right across 500 stocks.

              Most people underperform because they're too diversified. They're afraid of being wrong. We're not.
```

## 9. On Her Career

**Q: How did you develop your investment philosophy?**

```
Cathie Wood ❯  I started at Capital Group in 1977. At Jennison, I was a young analyst who didn't get first pick of what to cover. I ended up with internet and wireless stocks that nobody else wanted.

              That's where I learned to see value in emerging technologies before they were obvious.

              Then at AllianceBernstein for 12 years, I managed $5 billion in thematic strategies. But they weren't willing to take the leaps I wanted to take. So I left in 2014 and started ARK.

              I was 57 years old. Most people don't start companies at 57. But I had a mission.
```
