## 1. MIT Talk: "My Guiding Principles" (2010)

**Source:** [Simons Foundation](https://www.simonsfoundation.org/2020/01/22/my-guiding-principles/)

In late 2010, just after stepping down from Renaissance, Simons gave a talk at MIT. His wife Marilyn suggested he end the talk by discussing his values — which became his famous "Five Guiding Principles."

### The Five Guiding Principles

> **1. DO SOMETHING NEW; DON'T RUN WITH THE PACK.**
> "I am not such a fast runner. If I am one of N people all working on the same problem, there is very little chance I will win. If I can think of a new problem in a new area, that will give me a chance."

> **2. SURROUND YOURSELF WITH THE SMARTEST PEOPLE YOU CAN FIND.**
> "When you see such a person, do all you can to get them on board. That extends your reach, and terrific people are usually fun to work with."

> **3. BE GUIDED BY BEAUTY.**
> "This is obviously true in doing mathematics or writing poetry, but it is also true in fashioning an organization that is running extremely well and accomplishing its mission with excellence."

> **4. DON'T GIVE UP EASILY.**
> "Some things take much longer than one initially expects. If the goal is worth achieving, just stick with it."

> **5. HOPE FOR GOOD LUCK!**

### Key Excerpts from the Full Talk

On building the Simons Foundation's autism research program:
> "Our mission seemed clear: Attract great scientists, and begin with genetics. This decision was consistent with principle 1, since no one to our knowledge was taking this combined approach."

On the creation of the Simons Center for Data Analysis:
> "I liked this idea very much, since the wealth I had amassed was based on data science in the financial world, but instead of creating an institute on a university campus, I thought to assemble such an activity in-house."

## 2. TED Interview: "The Mathematician Who Cracked Wall Street" (2015)

**Source:** [TED Talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/jim_simons_the_mathematician_who_cracked_wall_street)

Simons' only TED appearance, a rare 23-minute interview discussing his three lives as mathematician, investor, and philanthropist.

On the Chern-Simons invariants:
> "That work was good mathematics. I was very happy with it; so was Chern. It even started a little subfield that's now flourishing. Today, those things in there called Chern-Simons invariants have spread through a lot of physics. And it was amazing. We didn't know any physics. It never occurred to me that it would be applied to physics. But that's the thing about mathematics — you never know where it's going to go."

On Math for America:
> "Instead of beating up the bad teachers, which has created morale problems all through the educational community, in particular in math and science, we focus on celebrating the good ones and giving them status."

## 6. Anecdote: The Strip Mall Beginning

**Source:** [Young & Calculated Substack](https://youngandcalculated.substack.com/p/the-story-of-renaissance-technologies)

In 1978, at age 40, Simons left academia and started a hedge fund out of a Long Island strip mall next to a women's clothing boutique and a pizza restaurant. He named it Monemetrics. He had no finance background and did not know how to read a balance sheet.

Early on, he traded currencies on intuition and lost sleep over it:
> "I don't want to have to worry about the market every minute. I want models that will make money while I sleep. A pure system without humans interfering."

This visceral discomfort with discretionary trading became the catalyst for the quantitative revolution at Renaissance.

## 7. Anecdote: The August 2007 Crisis

**Source:** [Daniel Scrivner](https://www.danielscrivner.com/renaissance-technologies-business-breakdown/)

In August 2007, mortgage markets were imploding. Renaissance's Medallion Fund lost $1 billion — 20% of its value — in three days. The partners gathered in Jim Simons' smoke-filled office, ready to override their computers and start selling.

**What happened:** They didn't touch anything. The models kept trading. By year-end, Medallion was up **85.9%** .

On this decision not to intervene, Peter Brown later said:
> "We're right 50.75% of the time, but we're 100% right 50.75% of the time. You can make billions that way."

## 8. Anecdote: The IBM Speech Team (1993)

**Source:** [Young & Calculated Substack](https://youngandcalculated.substack.com/p/the-story-of-renaissance-technologies)

Nick Patterson, a former NSA cryptographer at Renaissance, read that IBM was conducting layoffs. He knew the IBM Research speech recognition group contained some of the most technically sophisticated computational scientists working anywhere.

Patterson went recruiting and brought Peter Brown and Robert Mercer to Renaissance from IBM in 1993. Their background: natural language processing, hidden Markov models for speech-to-text, large-scale statistical inference, and machine translation.

When Brown and Mercer arrived, they argued for **one unified model** across all asset classes, rather than separate models for currencies, commodities, and equities. This became a turning point for the firm. The argument was structural:
> "A correlation discovered in soybeans might apply to the Japanese yen at certain volatility levels. You would only find that if all the data lived in one place and all the researchers saw the same model."

## 9. Anecdote: The Hiring Philosophy

**Source:** [The Billionaire Story Substack](https://thebillionairestory.substack.com/p/jim-simons-the-man-who-turned-math)

Renaissance notably passed over MBAs and finance PhDs. Simons' rationale:
> "We learned it's easier to teach smart people about markets than to teach market people how to be smart."

Their best performers came from IBM's speech recognition team, from astrophysics programs, from code-breaking agencies. One partner spent months typing French verb conjugations into a computer despite not speaking French.

## 13. Anecdote: The Cosmic Merger (2014)

**Source:** [Simons Foundation](https://www.simonsfoundation.org/2020/03/23/a-cosmic-merger-over-vegan-sprouts/)

Brian Keating's account of how the Simons Observatory was born. Over lunch at Princeton, Simons proposed merging two competing CMB experiments.

The conversation went:
> "Why don't you and Adrian join forces with ACT and take out the competition?"

When Keating estimated the cost at $50 million, Simons didn't blink:
> "If I gave a dollar to each person who told me I'm a genius, even I'd be broke."

On the timeline for discovering cosmic origins:
> "Let's make it closer to 10 [years]."

## 15. Anecdote: On Publicity

**Source:** Wikipedia / Zuckerman's "The Man Who Solved the Market"

Simons shunned the limelight and rarely gave interviews. When asked why, he quoted Benjamin the Donkey from George Orwell's *Animal Farm*:
> "God gave me a tail to keep off the flies. But I'd rather have had no tail and no flies. That's kind of the way I feel about publicity."

## 19. Forbes Last Interview — Forbes 400 Summit on Philanthropy (September 2023)

**Source:** [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/hanktucker/2024/05/10/billionaire-jim-simons-last-interview-the-hedge-fund-legend-on-the-universe-making-money-and-giving-it-away/)

This was Simons' last public appearance, at the 11th Annual Forbes 400 Summit on Philanthropy in New York. He and Marilyn were presented with the Forbes 400 Lifetime Achievement Award for Philanthropy. The interview was published posthumously on May 10, 2024, the day of his death.

### On the Foundation and His Role

> "I just made the money and Marilyn gave it away."

Despite the quip, in his later years Simons committed himself more personally to their charity. The couple has given more than **$6 billion** away via the Simons Foundation.

### On the $500 Million Gift to Stony Brook (2023)

> "Stony Brook means a lot to us. The department was only so-so, but [Nelson] Rockefeller was governor and he loved the state universities, so I had all the money I needed to build up the math department, which I did."

### On Meeting Marilyn Simons (Humorous Anecdote)

> "My ex-wife was going to Europe for the summer, so I had to take care of our three children, so the university sent someone over to see if she would do that job. We talked and we talked and we talked, and finally I said, 'Do you have a boyfriend?' And she said no, and the rest is history."

### On Math Education and Teacher Shortages

> "The whole economy is more and more dependent on quantitative skills, and we're behind in teaching them."

> "If you know enough math today to teach in high school, then you probably know enough to work for Google or Goldman Sachs or Renaissance Technologies. They pay a whole lot more than high school teaching. So, that means that not so many people who know the subject are going to go into that field, the field of teaching."

### On Autism Research

> "We have a team of people at the foundation which spends $100 million a year working on autism and understanding it better. It's not the only thing the foundation does of course, but it's one of the important things that it does."

### On the Origin of the Universe (Simons Observatory)

Simons discussed the Simons Observatory telescopes at 17,000 feet in Chile's Atacama Desert:

> "The conventional thinking is that the universe started with a point, with one point, and then hugely expanded, which is called inflation. If that's true, that big expansion would cause gravitational waves. The first thing we're going to do with this telescope is see, are there really primordial gravitational waves right at the beginning?"

> "Personally, I kind of hope we don't find these gravitational waves because I don't believe the universe started with a point. I think the universe has gone on way before anything. But we'll see."

This quote is extraordinary because it reveals Simons' personal cosmology — a belief that the universe is eternal rather than having a beginning. Even at the end of his life, he was willing to bet against the prevailing scientific consensus.

## 20. Simons Foundation Interview: On His Career in Mathematics (2012)

**Source:** [Simons Foundation](https://www.simonsfoundation.org/2012/09/28/simons-foundation-chair-jim-simons-on-his-career-in-mathematics/)

### The Garden Supply Store Anecdote

At age 14, Simons got a vacation job in the stockroom of a garden supply store. He was so bad at remembering where things went that he was quickly demoted to floor sweeper. When asked about his future plans:

> "The guy who couldn't remember where to put the sheep manure, he wants to be a mathematician at MIT."

### On Falling in Love with Mathematics

> "I didn't think it was math, just something fun to do."

> "I think there's a quality of just being able to get lost in thought that is pretty good for math."

### The MIT Café Moment — Deciding to Be a Mathematician

Simons described watching two MIT mathematicians, Isadore Singer and Warren Ambrose, launch into a deep discussion late at night at a café:

> "I thought it was the coolest thing — what a life, to go out at 2 a.m. with friends and do math over coffee. It seemed like the world's greatest career."

### On the IDA Codebreaking Experience

> "I think if I hadn't been there, the business we built later would never have occurred."

### On Mathematics and Physics

On the post-Chern-Simons intertwining of mathematics and physics:

> "Now they are inextricably intertwined, as far as I can see."

### On the Decision to Go Full Systematic (1988)

> "[Using mathematical models exclusively] turned out to be a wonderful decision."
