# 06-timeline.md — Jim Simons' Life & Career Timeline

> All facts verified. Sources noted.

---

## Era 1: Early Life & Education (1938–1961)

### 1938: Birth
- **April 25, 1938:** Born in Cambridge, Massachusetts (raised in Brookline)
- **Family:** Only child of Marcia (née Kantor) and Matthew Simons; middle-class Jewish family; father worked in a shoe factory
- **Early traits:** Would pass the time doubling numbers and pondering Zeno's paradox

### ~1944–1955: Childhood Fascination
- Age 5+ started showing mathematical aptitude
- "I liked everything about math. The only thing I thought about was that I would be a mathematician."

### 1958: MIT
- **B.S. in Mathematics** from MIT — completed in three years
- After graduation: rode a motor scooter from Boston to Bogotá, Colombia with a friend; at times asked police to sleep in local jails

### 1961: UC Berkeley PhD
- **Ph.D. in Mathematics** from UC Berkeley under Bertram Kostant — at age **23**
- Thesis: "On the Transitivity of Holonomy Systems"
- Gave a new proof of Berger's classification of holonomy groups of Riemannian manifolds

## Era 2: Codebreaking & Academic Career (1961–1978)

### 1961–1964: Early Teaching
- Brief teaching: MIT and Harvard University

### 1964–1968: NSA Codebreaker at IDA
- **Institute for Defense Analyses** (IDA), Princeton, New Jersey
- Worked on cryptographic problems for the National Security Agency
- Split time 50/50: cryptography and independent mathematical research
- Tried starting a trading company called iStar with colleagues (discovered by management, effort failed)
- **1973:** IBM asked Simons to attack block cipher Lucifer (early precursor to DES)

### 1967: Vietnam War Protest
- Wrote a letter to the editor of *The New York Times* opposing the Vietnam War

### 1968: Fired from IDA
- Told a *Newsweek* reporter he would stop working on Defense Department tasks until the war ended
- **Fired** by General Maxwell Taylor

### 1968–1978: Stony Brook University
- Became chair of the mathematics department at Stony Brook University
- Built what mathematician Isadore Singer called the best mathematics and physics department in the world
- Recruited brilliant mathematicians — the management blueprint for Renaissance

### 1974: Chern–Simons Form
- Published "Characteristic Forms and Geometric Invariants" with Shiing-Shen Chern
- Became foundational in string theory, condensed matter physics, and topological quantum field theory
- Described by Simons as "the high spot of my mathematical life"

### 1976: Oswald Veblen Prize
- Awarded by the American Mathematical Society in Geometry
- Also: Simons' formula and Simons cone contributions

### 1977: Marriage to Marilyn
- Met Marilyn Hawrys at Stony Brook
- Marilyn earned her Ph.D. in economics from Stony Brook in 1984

## Era 3: Renaissance Technologies (1978–2009)

### 1978: Monemetrics Founded
- **Age 40:** Left academia to start a hedge fund
- Located in a **strip mall** in East Setauket, Long Island — next to a women's clothing boutique and a pizza restaurant
- Initially traded currencies on macroeconomic intuition — described as making him "sick to his stomach"

### 1982: Renaissance Technologies
- Firm renamed to Renaissance Technologies — "rebirth"
- Began hiring mathematicians: Leonard E. Baum, James Ax
- Ax spun out into Axcom Ltd. (Simons retained 25% stake)

### 1988: Medallion Fund Launched
- Flagship fund established
- Returned only 9% in first year

### 1989: The Berlekamp Pivot
- **-4% loss** in second year
- Hired Elwyn Berlekamp from UC Berkeley to overhaul the system
- Shifted to shorter-term trades with Kelly Criterion position sizing
- **Result: 59% net return in 1989**

### 1990–1993: Early Medallion Returns
- 1990: +78%
- 1991: +54%
- 1992: +47%
- 1993: Medallion closes to outside investors

### 1993: IBM Speech Team Hired
- Nick Patterson recruits Robert Mercer and Peter Brown from IBM
- They bring hidden Markov models, large-scale statistical inference, machine translation
- **Unified model** architecture implemented — one model, all asset classes

### Mid-1990s: Expansion into Equities
- Medallion moves into equity markets
- Deeper liquidity allows scale without destroying edge

### 1994: Simons Foundation Established
- Founded with wife Marilyn
- Initially unfocused; became dedicated to mathematics and science research

### 1996: Personal Tragedy — Son Paul Dies
- Son Paul Simons, age 34, killed in a car accident while riding a bicycle in Long Island

### 2000: 128% Return Year
- Medallion returned +128% in a year the S&P 500 lost ~10%
- Fund at ~$2 billion under management

### 2003: Medallion Expels All Outside Investors
- Kicked out every external LP
- Fund became employee-only
- Son Nicholas Simons, age 24, drowned on a trip to Bali, Indonesia

### 2004: Math for America Founded
- Nonprofit to improve math education in U.S. public schools
- 1,000 STEM teachers supported annually in NYC

### 2005–2007: Peak Performance
- 2006: Est. $1.7 billion personal earnings (largest compensation among hedge fund managers)
- 2007: Medallion returns +136%; August crisis loss recovered
- 2008: +152% (S&P 500: -38.5%)

### 2009: Retirement Announced
- **October 10, 2009:** Announced retirement as CEO, effective January 1, 2010
- Remained as nonexecutive chairman of Renaissance

## Era 4: Philanthropy & Cosmology (2010–2024)

### 2010: MIT Speech — Five Guiding Principles
- Gave talk at MIT on "Mathematics, Common Sense, and Good Luck: My Life and Careers"
- Wife Marilyn suggested he discuss his values → the Five Guiding Principles were born:
  1. Do something new; don't run with the pack
  2. Surround yourself with the smartest people
  3. Be guided by beauty
  4. Don't give up easily
  5. Hope for good luck

### 2012: Simons Collaborations Created
- Peter Spergel recruited from Princeton
- Origins of Life and Global Brain collaborations launched
- Idea of Flatiron Institute conceived

### 2014: Chern–Simons Anniversary
- 40 years since the Chern–Simons paper
- Forbes headline: "Billionaire Jim Simons' Last Interview: The Hedge Fund Legend On The Universe, Making Money And Giving It Away"

### 2015: TED Interview
- Rare public appearance — "The mathematician who cracked Wall Street"
- Discussed his three lives: mathematician, investor, philanthropist

### 2016: Flatiron Institute Opens
- In-house computational science research division in New York City
- Directors: David Spergel (Astrophysics), Leslie Greengard (Applied Math)
- Asteroid 6618 Jimsimons named after him

### 2017: New Yorker Profile
- Major profile by *The New Yorker* titled "Jim Simons, the Numbers King"

### 2019: "The Man Who Solved the Market" Published
- Gregory Zuckerman's definitive biography of Simons and Renaissance
- Bestseller — brought Simons' story to a mainstream audience

### 2020: Pandemic Year
- Medallion returned +76% net (~149% gross)
- Simons Foundation launched Simons Observatory construction

### 2023: $500M Gift to Stony Brook
- **June 2023:** Simons Foundation announced $500 million gift to Stony Brook University endowment
- Largest unrestricted gift to an American university in history
- Net worth at ~$30 billion (Forbes 400: 25th richest in U.S.)

### 2024: Death
- **May 10, 2024:** Jim Simons dies at age 86 in New York City
- Net worth at death: $31.4 billion (55th richest globally)
- Survived by wife Marilyn, three children, five grandchildren, one great-grandchild

## Era 5: Legacy & Posthumous (2024–Present)

### 2024 (May): Simons Foundation Obituary
> "Jim (as he preferred to be called) was an award-winning mathematician, a legend in quantitative investing, and an inspired and generous philanthropist."

### 2025 (May): "Eternal Sky" Documentary Streaming
- Film about the Simons Observatory and Jim Simons' vision
- Directed by Debra Kellner
- Available for streaming via Simons Foundation website

### 2025 (Q4): Renaissance Portfolio Analysis
- Renaissance Technologies portfolio value: **$62.9 billion** as of December 31, 2025
- Continued active management by Robert Mercer, Peter Brown, and others

### 2025–2026: Ongoing MIT Simons Lectures
- The Jim & Marylin Research Fund in Mathematics continues funding annual lectures at MIT
